    Monty Hall Simulation (Pitt Project 1) >

    What:

    For this project, we had to make a program that would simulate the Monty Hall problem (see below for description). It would start with asking how many rounds the user wanted to simulate—between 10 and 10000. Then, it would ask the user if they wanted to switch or stay. Lastly, it would run the simulation and print out how many rounds the player won.

    The Monty Hall problem is a probability puzzle based on a game show scenario. You are presented with three doors: behind one door is a car (the prize you want), and behind the other two doors are goats. You choose one door, but before it’s opened, the host (who knows what’s behind each door) opens another door, revealing a goat. You are then given the option to stick with your original choice or switch to the remaining unopened door. The counterintuitive solution is that switching doors actually gives you a 2/3 chance of winning the car, while sticking with your original choice only gives you a 1/3 chance.

    Language/Tools Used:

    Python, Random library, Coded in Replit

    Visual Demo:

    (Video Link)

    What I Learned:

    • How to use list comprehension in Python
    • The probability theory behind the Monty Hall problem
    • Basic concepts of probability and statistics


    Reflection

    This project taught me more about probabilities. After finishing the project, me and a friend–James McCarter–had a race to see who could optimize and shrink down the code the most. In the end, I won. Using list comprehension, which I had taught myself how to use with no material, I got the code from ~120 lines of code down to 1.

    Now, some of the code was unneeded, like print statements to show a door making a GUI. However, I still think of this as an achievement as I had only been programming for a few months and managed to learn list comprehension solely through experimenting, implemented it into my program, and finish optimizing the code, all within half an hour. Though, the code is not very readable as one line and I acknowledge that it isn't very practical. However, it was still a fun challenge and I learned a lot from it, so I consider it an achievement.

    Sword Fighting >
    Like with technology, I've always loved sword fighting. I used to play with a bunch of sticks when I was little and loved doing it. My favorite time was at a Tekko convention where they had 1-on-1 fights and group fighting activities. 

    I was dual-wielding swords and fighting against a spear guy. He was the first spear person I had fought, so I was a bit inexperienced at first.  I had been able to just barely get a point by dashing towards him and hitting him before he could react. However, he was ready the next match and hit me before I could get close enough, making the score 2:2. And since whoever won this point would win, stakes were high. I had already used my one trick, so this time, I had to predict where he would strike and block while dashing towards him to win. 

    The match had concluded, and the judge was confused because, according to him, he had blinked and the match was done.  I still remember dashing towards my opponent while trying to block, and his spear just barely grazing my hair, making it fly to the side, and the wind from his spear literally blowing off the sweat from my face. And yet, my sword had hit his stomach. 

    Me, my enemy, the judge, and everyone else stood frozen at what just happened. We had to get another judge and question some of the crowd, as no one knew what had truly happened. I had told them about his spear grazing my head, as it could've counted as a kill . And finally, after a wait that felt like forever, the judges came to a decision. They said that due to the head strike likely not being enough to kill someone, I had won the match. 

    I remember me and my opponent talking after the fight and us agreeing that, had I not been able to perfectly predict and block his attack, I would've lost more than just the match.
